Introduction


If you cast your mind back to the start of 2020 and the fires that terrorised an alarming portion of Australia, this is but one clear sign that climate change is still a very real challenge, even if it is veiled beneath the current pandemic situation.


Undeniably one of the greatest challenges of the 21st century, Climate Change is a matter of global concern. As a result, numerous countries around the world have begun to make preventing climate change their number 1 priority.




Unfortunately, Australia is not one of such nations. Having stalled and resisted commitment to targets consistently, Australiaâ€™s climate change policy leaves much to be desired. However, if climate change is so serious why does Australia keep being allowed to stall, and exactly how bad is it?


In this visualisation I aim to explore these questions by considering some of the common excuses used to delay climate action, providing a look into what must be done, and how that may be achieved.



Heads in the Sand



Raw Emissions per Country


The climate change we are talking about here is in fact more specifically anthropogenic climate change; or put simply the type of climate change caused by human actions. This includes things such as transport, farming and production.

The common measure of which, is CO2 emissions, with the over abundance of CO2 thanks to humans causing additional heating to the natural greenhouse effect.


To the right you can see raw CO2 emissions per country, going back to 1919. While global emissions has consistently risen since 1919, it is interesting to look at how the biggest contributors to CO2 emissions change over time; or rather don't change much.

Over the last 100 years, the global super powers that are China and the USA are consistently first and second when it comes to raw emissions.

By comparison, when looking at Australia, we have remained consistent among the lower ranking emitters.


Emissions per Capita


However, once you bring population into the equation (see figure to the left), you quickly see quite the opposite, with Australia having the 9th highest emissions per capita globally.


The more concerning part, is that typically only the first half of this picture is drawn when it comes to what is shown in the media. This means that, for so long, Australia has got away with delaying climate action due to their proportion of global emissions.

By instead blaming China and America, Australia is able to hide behind the logic that nothing we do matters.

When looking at emissions per capita however, China and America are ranked 36 and 10 respectively, and this argument falls through immediately, when in fact the contrary is true.

The Need for Change



Relative Change in Emissions

This is all well and good to see that our per capita emissions are amongst the highest in the world, but what exactly are we supposed to do about?

Well, consider other countries such as France and Germany in the left figure. In comparison to Australia, these European countries have been making consistent efforts to decrease CO2 emissions in agreement with the Paris Agreement of 2015.

On the other end of the spectrum, countries such as China and India continue to increase year-on-year. However, looking at India between the year of 2012 and 2019, you can see a steady downward trend.

While Australia sees the year-on-year increases from China and India, we are all too happy to ignore the steady decreasing trend and excuse ourselves from needing to change as we are 'already ahead'.



Australian Emission Projections

This means that when it comes time to commit to meeting targets, while it may seem as though we can continue on as usual.

In reality, this could not be further from the truth.

Instead, we find ourselves at a critical point in time, whereby continuing on 'Business as Usual' may result in detrimental temperature increases depending on how far above the defined global target "Making Progress".
